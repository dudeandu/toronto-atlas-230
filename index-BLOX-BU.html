Mapping Toronto

Toronto is 230 years old, these nine maps tell you who we are today


Sunday marks 230 years since what is now Toronto was settled by the British on lands that were part of a disputed purchase from the Mississaugas of the New Credit First Nation (a purchase later clarified in 1805’s Treaty 13, which itself was subject to dispute until a claim settlement in 2010). On August 27th 1793, Lt.-Gov. John Graves Simcoe founded what was initially called the Town of York as Upper Canada’s capital. We’ve changed a lot from the forested lakeside wilderness of that time, growing into a city of 3 million. To mark the occasion, the Star created nine maps that provide a snapshot of how those millions live together today — from income to crime to demographics and growth, they illuminate the city we are, and the city we’re becoming.

Population density in an increasingly crowded city

We aren’t evenly spread out. It’s no surprise the downtown core is up to 15 times more dense than the single-family subdivisions of Etobicoke and Scarborough. But check out the even more densely populated downtown North York (with as many as 45,222 people per sq. km.), just blocks away from subdivisions with fewer than 2,200 per sq. km., illustrating the extreme contrasts that have emerged in neighbourhood development.

Growing in places, shrinking in others

Toronto’s growth is also unevenly developing. Predictably, the neighbourhoods (downtown, midtown, uptown) where highrise condos have become defining landmarks have been growing fastest. But what’s interesting and maybe troubling is that in a growing city with a housing crisis, the population along much of the Bloor subway line has actually been shrinking.

A city that’s mostly getting older

Toronto’s population growth isn’t coming from the birth of children, as the population of many areas — particularly the most established and wealthiest — has been getting older. Toronto’s “Benjamin Button” neighbourhood, where the average population has dropped more than 37 years since 2016, is near Victoria Park and the 401— leading just a handful of pockets spread across the city getting more youthful.

The income-divide doughnut

This map shows a red-hot midtown core on either side of Yonge Street, where the most affluent residents are clustered in and between Rosedale and the Bridle Path as if they’re filling a hole in a doughnut. That the city north of the 401 is home to people of more modest means is well-known, but the divide between affluent central Etobicoke and lower-income central Scarborough is stark.

A city of newcomers, particularly in the north

Toronto is a city of immigrants, though comparing where the highest concentrations of first-generation Canadians live in this map with the income map — north of the 401, or central Scarborough — paints a picture of newcomers still finding their feet economically. Part of north Agincourt is almost 95 per cent first-generation Canadians; at the other extreme, more than 85 per cent of one pocket of the Kingsway were born in Canada.

There are fewer places to buy a home for less than $1 million

Housing prices paint a picture similar to the income map: midtown neighbourhoods top $2 million, while many sub-million-dollar areas cluster in the northeast and northwest. Another apparently affordable area is in the downtown core, but condos there are measured in dozens of square feet and have maintenance fees. It may surprise that the Scarborough Bluffs pocket has pricier homes than the area including the Beaches.

To find the greenest parts of the city,
follow the money

The Humber and Don River valleys really stick out on the map of Toronto’s forests, as does the massive and gorgeous Scarborough ravine system and the vast expanse of Rouge Park. What a closer look might reveal, however, is the degree to which on a street-by-street basis the wealthiest central areas of the city are greener — in the nature sense, not just the dollar sense — than the lower-income areas, particularly in the northwest.

Toronto’s political divide evolves

The geographic divide between Olivia Chow voters and those of runner-up Ana Bailão tells the story of a new mayor ushered into office by a coalition of downtown and Scarborough residents, with the wealthier areas of midtown and central Etobicoke most interested in Bailão. It’s a different political divide than in the Rob Ford era, and perhaps a picture of who wanted a change of direction versus who supported the status quo.

Who can afford to live farther from the gunshots?

This map charts every reported firearm discharge over a decade. The downtown core shows the densest clusters of incidents, which you might expect given the city’s business and nightlife are concentrated in those areas. Other trends are harder to discern, except that even a glance shows that the wealthiest areas of the city seem somewhat insulated from a phenomenon troubling much of Toronto.
